The Problem

Offshore Integrity Still Runs
on Disconnected Workflows

The offshore asset integrity lifecycle — inspection, engineering assessment, and maintenance — still depends on manual workflows, fragmented tooling, and subjective engineering judgment applied inconsistently across disciplines, assets, and teams.

Structural findings live in PDF reports. Mechanical assessments exist in spreadsheets. Piping, electrical, and instrumentation data sit in separate systems entirely. None of these share context across disciplines, and none of them learn.

01
Manual Anomaly Assessment

Inspectors across disciplines photograph anomalies and classify severity based on experience. Two inspectors assessing the same finding — whether a corroded beam, a leaking flange, or a degraded cable tray — may reach different conclusions.

02
Disconnected Engineering Analysis

Inspection data flows into separate engineering systems — if it flows at all. Assessments against standards happen in isolation, with no traceability from the original finding to the engineering conclusion.

03
Reactive Maintenance Planning

Maintenance scopes are built manually after campaigns complete. Work scopes are assembled from scratch without structured linkage to the findings they address.

04
No Shared Intelligence Layer

Inspection, engineering, and maintenance teams operate on different tools — and each discipline operates in its own silo. No unified system connects findings to assessments to work orders across all disciplines.
